Sustainability at the University of Maryland
by Emma Grazado

With sustainability on the rise, how does the University of Maryland prioritize limiting its environmental impact?

The University of Maryland is ranked in the top 40 “Cool Schools” by Sierra Magazine, for its exemplary performance in sustainable action, and it is ranked second among the Big 10.

The university has also been awarded a Gold ranking in the Sustainability Tracking, Assessment & Rating System (STARS) program by the Association for the Advancement of Sustainability in Higher Education (AASHE).

The Climate Action Plan (CAP) by the Office of Sustainability projects the following achievements, based on the 2005 baseline:
  • 50% reduction in greenhouse gas emissions by 2020
  • 60% reduction in greenhouse gas emissions by 2025
  • Carbon neutral by 2050

As sustainability gets more popular on campus, more and more involvement opportunities are available for students. Groups like MaryPIRG, Epsilon Eta, and the Sustainability Cooperative are changing the way students look at sustainability.

The Office of Sustainability has also worked with students on campus to provide programming to make sustainable actions a part of daily student life. Their most well-known initiative is the Green Terp program.

The Green Terp program has University of Maryland students complete a pledge to practice sustainable actions in each of the following categories: recycling, water conservation, energy conservation, transportation, consumption, and cleaning and personal care.

In the 2018-2019 school year, 1,075 students took the Green Terp pledge in which they chose their most common sustainable actions by category.

How green are our Terps?

Participants of the Green Terp program must complete a pledge to join, then certify after completing sustainable actions. The 1,075 students that certified identified which actions they took over the course of the academic year. They were required to choose one action from each category and 10 overall. On top of the Green Terp programming, the Resident Hall Association is working on implementing a GreenEdu training, mandatory for first-year residents. The program is modeled after AlcoholEdu, an online training residents must take to review responsible drinking habits.

By providing these types of programming to new Terps, the culture around sustainable living will be more normalized and expected, resulting in a lower environmental impact of the university.
